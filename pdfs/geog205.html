Course Description

            Geography 205 is an introductory course in physical geography that explores environmental change and the structures of knowledge around environmental topics. The purpose of this course is to apply a systems perspective on attributes, elements, and connections within the earth’s physical environment and to learn how and why the environment changes at multiple spatial and temporal scales. A systems approach is used to structure our approach to the atmosphere, cryosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere and biosphere. In addition to being a core curriculum course, it is a cultural discourse (CD) which entails discussion and reflection about the environment.

            The science of measuring environmental change is empirical. Repeated observations of patterns and phenomena lead to generalizations that allow scientists to better understand the processes that shape the world around them. The observations and measurements can be quantitative (air temperature), semi-quantitative (climate reconstruction based on fossilized pollen), or qualitative (descriptions of streams). Scientists then interpret these different types of data by analyzing them within the framework provided by previous studies on similar topics. Depending on the type of data they analyze, and the consensus on the validity of those datasets, scientists can make different conclusions on similar topics.

            We often think of scientific evidence and consensus as “high agreement, robust evidence,” but in practice most science questions regarding environmental change do not fall into this category. Model simulations are mathematical representations of the patterns and processes that have been observed and measured, which means that they are only as good as our understanding of these phenomena. In combination, empirical, experimental, and modeling studies allow for scientists to keep improving the state of our science; it also allows for discussion on alternative approaches and interpretation of the same data, which inevitably leads to discussion of power, bias, and privilege as influential in decision-making processes.

            Environmental change affects all people: locally and globally, with “people” meaning our collective structures. In addition to individuals, communities, societies, and governments also are partly involved in causing environmental change. These features make environmental change problems extremely complex and difficult problems to resolve. This is why this course will require students to engage in discussion regarding challenging topics connecting to environmental change. In doing so, students will be able to address how bias, power, intolerance, and conflict are linked to the processes and outcomes of environmental change.


 

Course Prerequisites

NONE.

There are no prerequisites for this course, so there is no presumption of prior geographical study. I assume all university students are capable and willing to learn. You must take responsibility for your learning by watching lectures, interacting with other students, completing assignments, reading textbooks, and above all else evaluating your own learning and trying to improve. Online assignments will require students to be able to read and understand scientific evidence, cite appropriate scientific data on environmental change, and understand natural processes, which will reinforce lecture material and explore the techniques used to identify and measure natural and anthropogenic changes to the geophysical environment at a range of scales. These assignments will also include reflection on the themes of bias and power to reinforce course discussions.

 

 

Special Course Designation

Core curriculum (CORE), and cultural discourse (CD)

Core: Life and Physical Sciences

Courses in this category focus on describing, explaining, and predicting natural phenomena using the scientific method. Courses involve the understanding of interactions among natural phenomena and the implications of scientific principles on the physical world and on human experiences.

The following skills will be addressed in the courses that comprise this area:

  • Critical thinking
    • Critical Thinking skills include creative thinking, innovation, inquiry, and analysis, evaluation, and synthesis of information. This core objective isrequired to be covered in all FCAs. 
  • Communication
    • Communication skills include effective development, interpretation, and expression of ideas through written, oral, and visual communication. Written, oral, and visual communication are treated as separate learning outcomes, and eachmust be covered in all FCAs. 
  • Empirical and quantitative
    • Empirical & Quantitative Skills include the manipulation and analysis of numerical data or observable facts resulting in informed conclusions. Empirical & Quantitative Skills must be covered in the following FCAs: Life & Physical Sciences, Mathematics, and Social & Behavioral Sciences. 
  • Teamwork
    • Teamwork includes the ability to consider different points of view and to work effectively with others to support a shared purpose or goal. Teamwork must be covered in the following Foundational Component Areas: Communication, Creative Arts, and Life & Physical Sciences. 

 

Cultural Discourse
Students completing their CD course requirement should be able to:

  1. Hold respectful discussions and discourse on difficult topics;
  2. Understand self, including personal bias and prejudices;
  3. Understand how power or authority is distributed within organizational systems, including recognizing potential forms of privilege, oppression, and discrimination;
  4. Understand how to use and promote informed dialogue to overcome issues dividing individuals and humankind;
  5. Understand tolerance and intolerance and have knowledge of when it is appropriate to be intolerant of specific behaviors/activities that violate our core values as people and Aggies;
  6. Understand how to function effectively in a multicultural and global society; and.
  7. Understand conflict from multiple viewpoints.

Courses with a CD designation should:

  1. Incorporate "Aggie Experience Activities" into the course; and
  2. Incorporate small group discussions into the course.

 

Course Learning Outcomes

  • Identify and describe the important attributes, elements and connections within the physical environment from a systems perspective.
  • Describe the dynamic nature of the environment at a range of spatial and temporal scales and how change results from adjustments between the different components.
  • Identify, explain, and evaluate methods and technology to measuring environmental change and recognize the limitations to these methods in predicting change relevant to a globalized society of people, communities, and governments.
  • Articulate how the differing effects of environmental change to people, communities, society, and governments are influenced by power, bias, and authority.
  • Demonstrate skills in engaging in small group dialogue, with particular attention to how to do this in the online environment.
  • Reflect on how bias and prejudice may influence subjective and scientific interpretations of environmental change.
  • Practice informed dialogue to understand diverse points of view and power imbalances that influence understandings of environmental change.

Grading Policy

  •          I use a total point scheme for grading. Every examination and exercise has a set value of points, and the course grade is determined from the total number of points accumulated. The point sources will consist of six major themes and activities; typically, one idea per two weeks, each one worth 150 points (or 15% of the grade) for a total of 750 points. Each of these are further divided into constituent parts: writing assignment, peer grading, response, quiz, and discussion. There is the Aggie Experience assignment that is required of “CD” courses and is also worth 125 points. There is one group assignment: outline. The final source of points are online quizzes: (6 quizzes worth 15+ points). The quizzes will be 24hr windows to complete as individuals.

    • The final grade is based on the total points and ranking of your point totals, not necessarily the percentage of points. The LMS (Canvas) calculation of percentages is not official for grades; it is useful feedback. There is no rounding up of points. For a typical section, 900/800/700/600 of the possible 1000 points can be used as an approximate guide for A/B/C/D thresholds. I reserve the right to modify the thresholds downward after evaluating the entire class and the point distribution.
      • A = 900-1000+ points
      • B = 800-899 points
      • C = 700-799 points
      • D = 600-699 points

    Failing = 0-599 points

     

                As an online course, a No Excuse Policy is applied to any activity or due date when the class has ample time to work ahead. Do not procrastinate! Work ahead and troubleshoot technological problems ahead of time. Any examination date or due-date missed is a tentative zero (see schedule). Excused absences are by university policy! For online assignments, an excused absence would need to cover the whole range of available dates and not just the due date. Exam day emergencies require immediate notification to an appropriate university official and prompt written verification of emergency.

    Graded Class Participation – Class participation is required for the seven group discussions. Each group will poll a time to conduct a zoom meeting and record their discussion. Each of the discussions is embedded into the weekly activities; they add up to approximately 175 points (or 17.5% of the course grade). There are no make-ups, and if a student fails to communicate with their group, the group moves on without them.

Exams – The online quizzes are generally scheduled during the second week of the two week cycle; the quizzes have a 24 hour window. Details will be announced in advance, but most likely midweek evenings.

Statement on the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA)

FERPA is a federal law designed to protect the privacy of educational records by limiting access to these records, to establish the right of students to inspect and review their educational records and to provide guidelines for the correction of inaccurate and misleading data through informal and formal hearings. Currently enrolled students wishing to withhold any or all directory information items may do so by going to howdy.tamu.edu and clicking on the "Directory Hold Information" link in the Student Records channel on the MyRecord tab. All efforts will be made in this class to protect your privacy and to ensure confidential treatment of information associated with or generated by your participation in the class.

The instructor will establish small online discussion groups of 4-6 random students for the duration of each unit. Students will be required to meet with their assigned group via Zoom. Your posts and comments must demonstrate: (1) your respect for the beliefs of others; (2) your ability to view conflict from multiple viewpoints; (3) your ability to promote informed dialogue; and (4) your ability to identify how power, authority, or oppression may shape multiple viewpoints.

Essays Due on Saturdays

Length should be 1-2 pages; ; approx. 500-750 words; citations can be on a third page. Formatting for ALL assignments should be 12pt, Arial or Helvetica, single or 1.5 spaced.  For each assignment, you must include at least one map, graph, or table from a credible, scientific publication in your narrative. The image should not take up more than half the page, with at least one page of text. Be sure to use at least 2 credible, scientific citations and include your citations in MLA, APA, or Turabian format. Check the library resources if you have additional questions; do not use footnote, endnotes, or hypertext links. After the Works Cited, a “writing statement” must be present that elaborates AI usage.

 

A properly submitted essay will have all the narrative writing and graphic fitting onto 2 pages; the third page should only be for works cited, appendix (if necessary), and AI clarifications. A high-quality essay has a good title. No author name or identifying information should be visible inside the document; no page numbers are necessary.

ESSAY PROMPTS

Assignment 1: Start of the Anthropocene 

Write a persuasive essay either agreeing or disagreeing with the year 1950 being chosen as the start date of the Anthropocene. If you disagree with 1950 as the start date of the Anthropocene, choose another date and explain why. If you agree with 1950 being the best start date of the Anthropocene, explain why. Your argument must be based on scientific understandings supported from the scientific peer-reviewed literature and by charts, tables, graphs, or other material.

No use of Artificial Intelligence and/or large language models for the first essay, you must submit an unaltered writing sample that only uses basic text processing abilities such as spell check. Any evidence of plagiarism or AI will go straight to honor code investigation.

Assignment 2: Biosphere Change  

Write an essay containing multiple perspectives about a biosphere change example (e.g. forest decline, invasive species invasion, species extinction, land use/land cover change). How has power, authority, or oppression shaped these statements/tables/ graphs/etc. How might people who have suffered directly from these biosphere changes offer an alternative view-based power, authority, or oppression? Your essay must reflect on how your own biases and prejudices may influence your interpretations of biosphere changes.

Assignment 3: Hydrosphere  

Write an essay which revolves around a hydrosphere change example (e.g. surface water quality or quantity, aquifer dynamics, flooding, reservoirs, glacial decline), contains multiple perspectives, and proposes an idea to unify the opposing perspectives. Your thesis statement should aim to unify the opposing views of government, industry, and citizens toward a common goal. Your critique must be based on scientific understandings of the hydrosphere/cryosphere, supported by charts, tables, graphs, or other material from the scientific peer-reviewed literature. Your essay must reflect on how your own biases and prejudices may influence your interpretations of hydrosphere changes.  

Assignment 4: Atmosphere (climate change — policy brief)

You will create a 1-2 page policy brief for decision makers endorsing one mitigation measure as defined by the IPCC relating to climate change as proposed in a peer-reviewed scientific paper. Your policy brief must recognize how people, communities, societies, and governments would benefit from, or be harmed by, the mitigation measure by specifically addressing a scientific measure or hypothesis relating to climate change. Your statement must define the problem scientifically, using relevant and appropriate facts, and then create a statement that recognizes “winners” and “losers” in the near, medium, and long term. Specifically, your policy statement must indicate how globalization and a global society inform your proposed mitigation measure.  

Assignment 5: Lithosphere (terrain change example -- local) 

Write an essay which revolves around a lithosphere change topic, for example terrain forming, beach restoration, etc. and with an eye towards something you can observe in the local environment (and take a photograph of it). If you cannot take a photograph of your topic, include an internet or other person’s image of the topic. Write a persuasive essay that approves or disapprove of the documented change. Who benefits? Who pays the costs? Your essay topic might have some personal preference or bias in what you see or experience; support your perspective with scientific understandings from two peer-reviewed sources and further supported by charts, tables, graphs, or other material from the scientific literature. Your essay must reflect on how your own biases and prejudices may influence your interpretations of lithosphere changes.

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) Policy

Texas A&M University is committed to providing equitable access to learning opportunities for all students. If you experience barriers to your education due to a disability or think you may have a disability, please contact Disability Resources office on your campus (resources listed below). Disabilities may include, but are not limited to attentional, learning, mental health, sensory, physical, or chronic health conditions. All students are encouraged to discuss their disability related needs with Disability Resources and their instructors as soon as possible.

Title IX and Statement on Limits to Confidentiality

Texas A&M University is committed to fostering a learning environment that is safe and productive for all. University policies and federal and state laws prohibit gender-based discrimination and sexual harassment, including sexual assault, sexual exploitation, domestic violence, dating violence, and stalking.

With the exception of some medical and mental health providers, all university employees (including full and part-time faculty, staff, paid graduate assistants, student workers, etc.) are Mandatory Reporters and must report to the Title IX Office if the employee experiences, observes, or becomes aware of an incident that meets the following conditions (see University Rule 08.01.01.M1):

  • The incident is reasonably believed to be discrimination or harassment.
  • The incident is alleged to have been committed by or against a person who, at the time of the incident, was (1) a student enrolled at the University or (2) an employee of the University.

Mandatory Reporters must file a report regardless of how the information comes to their attention – including but not limited to face-to-face conversations, a written class assignment or paper, class discussion, email, text, or social media post. Although Mandatory Reporters must file a report, in most instances, a person who is subjected to the alleged conduct will be able to control how the report is handled, including whether or not to pursue a formal investigation. The University’s goal is to make sure you are aware of the range of options available to you and to ensure access to the resources you need.

Statement on Mental Health and Wellness

Texas A&M University recognizes that mental health and wellness are critical factors influencing a student’s academic success and overall wellbeing. Students are encouraged to engage in healthy self-care practices by utilizing the resources and services available through University Health Services. Students needing a listening ear can call the Texas A&M Helpline (979.845.2700) from 4:00 p.m. to 8:00 a.m. weekdays and 24 hours on weekends for mental health peer support while classes are in session. The TELUS Health Student Support app provides access to professional counseling in multiple languages anytime, anywhere by phone or chat, and the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline offers 24-hour emergency support at 988 or 988lifeline.org Links to an external site..
